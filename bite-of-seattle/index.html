Make it Usable

First Impressions: A Homepage without Heirarchy

My student team kicked off this project with a heuristic audit of The Bite's existing website, noting opportunities for improvement. The festival's online presence has grown organically over the years, and this is especially clear on their jampacked homepage.

Mystery Menu Items

The Bite's information architecture was confusing to us, and the delineation between content categories was not entirely clear. It was clear from the jump that improving the navigation would be the backbone of our redesign.

Visual Design #Goals

During our team audit, I also set specific goals for myself to improve the site's usability by cleaning up the visual aesthetic:

  • Reduce cognitive load: The Bite's current site (especially the map) feels cluttered due to inconsistent styling of visual elements.
  • Introduce a type system: Every other line of text is emphasized, which makes it difficult for visitors to scan for meaning.
  • Mind the grid: Misaligned elements add to the sense of clutter and hurt readability — easily remedied with a grid system.

But First — Are We Solving the Right Problems?

To gather data on the website/festival visitor experience, we conducted interviews with volunteers who had attended Bite of Seattle (or similar events) in the past year. Following a set of scripted questions, we asked them to draw on memories (good and bad) of online event discovery, planning a trip through the event website, and physically navigating the event space.

One problem was immediatly obvious — none of the folks who'd attended The Bite before were aware of their special admission packages.

6 interviews, 10 questions, 30 minutes each

Turning Pain Points into Sweet Spots

My team's first priority was to improve the UX of planning a trip through The Bite's website, but we also wanted to consider its potential usefulness navigating a busy fairground. We synthesized our interview data as a team, with these goals in mind:

  • Find low hanging fruit: What are the lowest lift/highest impact improvements we can tackle during this design sprint?
  • Define business goals: The Bite is an imaginary client — what do we imagine are their desired outcomes for the redesign?

We uncovered the following four user experience categories to drive our redesign. Within each category, we hypothesized business objectives to benefit our 'client' Bite of Seattle.

Special Admission Packages

The Bite offers tiered admission packages for discounted food/drinks, which also benefit Seattle's Food Lifeline charity. Visitors are missing out on special packages because the details are convoluted/buried in the site navigation.

  • User need: Food/drink discounts, support a local charity
  • Business goal: Increase ticket sales/contributions to charity
IA & Taxonomy

Sections of The Bite's current site have mysterious and/or overly specific names. The difference between site categories is blurry.

  • User need: Answers to their questions regarding food menus, music schedules, parking... you name it
  • Business goal: Decrease bounce rates when users are unable to find the information they need quickly
Social Media Integration

We were surprised/encouraged by the prevalence of social media as a research tool for visitors. Lacking real analytics, we made some assumptions as to how increaseing social media integration might benefit The Bite.

  • User need: Look up participating restaurants and local bands before deciding to attend
  • Business goal: Increase word-of-mouth discovery, entice high caliber vendors by providing grassroots advertising
On-Site Navigation

A number of our volunteers admitted that they don't like using paper maps, and instead follow the crowd. A digital map on the mobile site could help visitors make informed descisions and (hopefully) reduce fairground congestion.

  • User need: A convenient alternative to the 'herd mentality'
  • Business goal: Increase sponsors’, vendors’, and performers’ profitability by dispersing crowds

The Bite is Unique — Calendars, Filters & Maps are Not

My team conducted a competitive analysis on other festivals/food service websites, taking note of established patterns we might use to improve the site's content organization and UX design.

Live Music

U District Street Fair | ACL

ACL performance lineup

Festival websites typically display performance schedules by day, because people purchase tickets based on the date — not where they'll be standing.

The Bite's entertainment section is currently grouped by location, with separate webpages for each stage.

Food Filters

Pike Place Market | Uber Eats

Search filters on Doordash

People choose what to eat by asking themselves questions. What am I in the mood for? What's available? What time of day is it? Search filters that align with those questions are intuitive to use.

Fairground Maps

Bonnaroo | ACL | Bumbershoot

Bonnaroo music festival map

Proximity, scale, and absolute distance are missing from fairground maps, because people WHAT WHAT WHAT.

Visual Themes
  • Abstraction over accuracy
  • Color-coded categories
  • Larger-than-life symbols to mark utilities and landmarks

Mood Board

Open Card Sort

Our information architect, Clarice, organized an open card sort to determine more intuitive top level navigation. She invited us all to join the exercise, plus 5 volunteers, for a total of 8 participants. We synthesized the card sort results as a team, and Clarice created a site map for our working prototype.

Local Color = Always on Brand

SHORTEN THIS UP. Seattle has a (well deserved) reputation for rainy skies most of the year but — believe it or not — our summers are stunning. Since The Bite is an outdoor summer festival, I pulled together a color pallete of bright primaries and sunny pastels. I named each shade after city landmarks and surrounding geography, for a seasonal color story specific to Seattle.

Unsplash Photo Credits

Retro-Futurist Type Studies

Chris proposed Mexcellent as a playful display font for headers — reminiscent of the Space Needle's debut at the 1962 World's Fair. I was jazzed about the trilinear font's layering capabilities (but would later scrap my 'colorpop' headers due to accessibility issues...). I tempered Mexcellent with Open Sans for body text — a typeface specifically designed for legibility across print, web, and mobile interfaces.

Imagine... a Better Map

According to our interview data, some folks really dislike paper maps — finding them wasteful, cumbersome, and easily misplaced. To improve The Bite's on-site experience, my team designed a digital map with a suite of interactive features for the mobile site. This map is environmentally friendly, pocket-sized, and unlikely to be misplaced.

✨ Clarice (IA) was the mastermind behind this dynamic map concept and QR scanning/pinning features. As the visual designer, I worked closely with her to bring the concept to life by creating animated mockups.

'You Are Here'

Guests can quickly get their bearings as they wander Seattle Center by scanning QR codes posted at each booth. The Bite would provide QR signage to festival vendors during the registration process.

Pinned Attractions

Guests who prefer to plan ahead can "pin" specific vendors to check out on event day. Pins can be shown/hidden by toggling the tab in the upper right, and the full list pins can be accessed from the key drawer.

Key Drawer & Gestures

To preserve space, the mobile map's legend and index are hidden off-screen in a bottom drawer. The map itself is a large image area that can be traversed with familiar gestures: pinch to zoom, swipe to scroll.

Compliance ≠ Acessibility

This is all very visual, because I'm thinking like a visual designer. But what about non-sighted users? Compliance and Acessibility - they're not the same thing. For example - I fixed my color contrast and font sizes, but that alone does nothing to improve the site for vision-impaired users plan their trip to The Bite.

Thoughtful Markup

Closing Thoughts

Feedback is a gift. Expound on that, and thank the designer who called you out. Feedback is a gift, and this feedback sent me on a journey to become a better designer. I embarked on self-learning web accessibility.

I also revisited the original ask: deisgn a responsive website, and realized that this doesn't cut it. It's not elastic. My mistake I think was that I made a style tile, and then rushed right from wireframes to comps. I made a series of rigid pages - not modular compnenets - so this desing is NOT truly responsive (yet).

Next Steps

  • The joy of constraints: I'll never not factor in accessibility at the start of my process again — what an expensive oversight, going back to change everything. This made me realize that accessibility wasn't the only constraint I hadn't considered — I also wasn't thinking about technical/platform constraints of what's possible to build.

  • We need a system for this: Make a style guide of replicable components - kick the habit of thinking in terms of pages! As I went back through the Sketch files, I realized I didn't do a very good job keeping the visual design CONSISTENT.

  • Unitless measurements: Diving deeper into responsive design, I learned that I would need to get comfy with SVG's, rems, ems, and percentages.
